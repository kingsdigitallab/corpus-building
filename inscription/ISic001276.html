commentary

The epigram probably accompanied a statue of Apollo. It consisted of two hexameters (Kaibel’s interpretation in Epigr. Gr. of an elegiac distichon is to be rejected), after which the name of the dedicant in prose should follow (Πατρ---). The epigram has epic echoes, such as the formula Φοῖβον ἀκερσεκόμην at l. 1 (cf. e.g. Hom. Il. 20.39, Hymn.Ap. 134) and the formula ἑκατηβόλον Ἀπόλλωνα at l. 2 (cf. e.g. Hom. Il. 1.370, 5.444, 16.711, 17.333): especially the first formula recurs in the variant Φοίβῳ ἀκειρεκόμῃ in other dedicatory epigrams on stone, cf. IG 4/2, 1 no.433 (= Epigr.ded. 212 Cougny) from Epidaurus (4th CE) and IGUR I no.102b (= Epigr.ded. 247 Cougny) from Rome (2nd-3rd CE).

The inscription is dated palaeographically to the 3rd-4th cent. CE (Manganaro’s dating to the Hellenistic age is to be rejected) and comes probably from Catania, even if the provenance is doubtful, since it was found in a group of inscriptions, of which at least one comes from Syracuse (IG 14, no.58 = ISic000875).
